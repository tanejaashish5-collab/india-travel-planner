### The "How It Works" section

> ## Three things we do. Nothing else.
>
> **We score.** Every destination on Nakshiq gets a number from 0 to 10 for every month, computed across six dimensions — weather, roads, crowds, safety, family suitability, permit complexity — and reviewed by a human before it's published.
>
> **We verify.** Road conditions are timestamped. Permits are tested. Homestay availability is confirmed within 30 days. If we can't verify it, we don't publish it.
>
> **We refuse to sell.** Nakshiq is not a booking platform. We don't take commissions. No destination pays to have its score raised. The day that rule breaks is the day this company dies.

### The "Why We Exist" section (for the About page)

> ## Why Nakshiq exists
>
> India has more travel content than any country on earth and almost none of it tells the truth. Tourist boards oversell. Blogs are SEO sludge. Google reviews are bot-farmed. Instagram is a highlight reel. A traveller who wants to drive through Spiti in October, take their kids to a monastery in Tawang, or cross the Himalayas on a bike has no single source that says with confidence: *here's what this place is actually like in this exact month, here's what the road is doing right now, here's whether your kids will hate it, here's the permit nobody mentions, here's the honest score.*
>
> Nakshiq is that source.
>
> We built it because the founder got tired of being the source — the friend who got messaged every two weeks with *"hey, is X worth it in Y month?"* and typed out a paragraph from memory each time. After fifteen years of writing those paragraphs, it became obvious they should exist somewhere public, structured, verified, and searchable. Now they do.
>
> **Go with confidence.**

## Part Four — Push Notification Copy

Push notifications are the single most easily-abused channel in a mobile app. Nakshiq's discipline: **no more than 2 push notifications per user per week, and every one of them has to be specific, useful, and true.** The moment Nakshiq sends a "we miss you!" push, the brand is dead.

### Approved push notification patterns

**Pattern 1 — Peak Window opening (for destinations on the user's watchlist)**
> **Spiti's window just opened.**
> The score moved from 6.1 to 9.2 today. Kunzum cleared.

**Pattern 2 — Peak Window closing (urgency)**
> **Six weeks left on Spiti.**
> The peak window closes mid-June. Here's what to do about it.

**Pattern 3 — Correction / honesty**
> **Correction: Gulmarg in June.**
> We over-scored it. New verdict: Skip. Here's why.

**Pattern 4 — The Window Monday delivery**
> **This week's Window is live.**
> Three to go to, two to skip. 4 min read.

**Pattern 5 — Field Note verification (thanks)**
> **Your Field Note was published.**
> Ananya K. is now a Nakshiq contributor. 47 people read it this week.

### Banned patterns

> ❌ "We miss you!"
> ❌ "Come back and explore!"
> ❌ "Your next adventure awaits!"
> ❌ "Don't miss out on exclusive deals!"
> ❌ "Limited time: X% off!"
> ❌ Any emoji salad
> ❌ Any push that doesn't respect the reader's time

## Part Eleven — The Founder Announcement Post (long-form)

For LinkedIn + Nakshiq blog + Substack cross-post on launch day.

> # I built an honest map of India. Here's why.
>
> For fifteen years, I was the friend everyone messaged before a trip.
>
> *"Hey, thinking of taking the kids to Mussoorie in October. Worth it?"*
> *"Is Spiti in April too early?"*
> *"What's the Ladakh permit situation this year?"*
> *"Is Gulmarg overrated?"*
>
> I'd type out a paragraph from memory each time. *Yeah, Mussoorie's fine in October but skip the first weekend — school holiday crush. The homestay I'd send you to is on the Landour side, not Mall Road. Wait till November if you can, the air is better.* I wrote that specific paragraph maybe forty times over the years. And every time, I thought: *this should exist. Not just for Mussoorie. For everywhere. With the same level of specificity.*
>
> That thing finally exists. It's called Nakshiq. It launched today.
>
> ## The problem
>
> India has more travel content than any country on earth and almost none of it tells the truth. Tourist boards oversell. Blogs are SEO sludge written by people who've never been there. Google reviews are bot-farmed. Instagram is a highlight reel that strips away every inconvenient detail. A traveller who wants to drive through Spiti in October, take their kids to a monastery in Tawang, or cross the Himalayas on a bike has no single source that says with confidence: *here's what this place is actually like in this exact month, here's what the road is doing right now, here's whether your kids will hate it, here's the permit nobody mentions, here's the honest score.*
>
> The industry is built around the moment of booking, not the moment of deciding. Every platform wants you to book something — a hotel, a flight, a package — because that's where the commissions live. Nobody's paid to tell you whether a trip is worth taking in the first place.
>
> ## The insight
>
> Here's the thing I kept coming back to: **the unit of intelligence is the month, not the destination.**
>
> Spiti in June is paradise. Spiti in October is a trip that gets cancelled because the road washed out. The same destination has twelve different identities across the year, and almost no platform captures this. Every destination is actually twelve destinations. And the decision a traveller is making is never *"should I go to Spiti?"* — it's *"should I go to Spiti in May or wait till September?"* That's the question nobody's answering at the level of rigour it deserves.
>
> ## What Nakshiq does
>
> Nakshiq scores 124 destinations across India on every month of the year. Each score is 0 to 10, computed from six dimensions:
>
> - **Weather suitability** — temperature, rainfall, sunshine
> - **Road conditions** — current state, verified in the last 30 days
> - **Crowd intensity** — how busy it actually is, including festival overlaps
> - **Safety profile** — area, road, altitude, specifically-for-solo-women considerations
> - **Family suitability** — is it kid-friendly, is it age-appropriate
> - **Permit and logistics complexity** — what you actually need to do before you can go
>
> Every score is reviewed by a human editor before it's published. Every claim has a timestamp. Every verdict is defensible.
>
> And — this is the rule I wrote on day one and will die defending — **no destination can pay to raise its score.** Not a rupee. Not ever. The day that rule breaks is the day the company is dead, and I'd rather shut it down than compromise it.
>
> ## What Nakshiq is not
>
> Nakshiq is not a booking platform. We don't sell flights. We don't sell hotels. We don't take commissions. We don't accept tourism board money to boost coverage. We publish Skip List editions where we tell people to *not* visit certain destinations — tourism boards will complain, and the complaint is the marketing.
>
> This is a weird business model for an Indian travel startup in 2026. Every other platform in the space makes its money from bookings. We're making a deliberate bet that the audience is tired of that, and will pay (eventually, via a premium tier) for a source that refuses to be incentivised to lie to them.
>
> If that bet is wrong, Nakshiq stays small. That's fine. It's not a unicorn business. It's not trying to be. It's an editorial business that believes the Indian premium traveller deserves the same rigour and independence that foreign travellers get from Monocle or Cereal, and that nobody has bothered to build it yet. We're building it.
>
> ## Who this is for

## Part Thirteen — The "Don't Screw This Up" Checklist

The top 10 things that could kill Nakshiq's brand in Year One, in the order of how easy they are to accidentally do.

1. **Accept even one piece of sponsored content.** Someone from a hill station tourism board will email you offering "a partnership where we provide destination content and you feature it." Delete the email. Do not reply. Do not explain.

2. **Change a Score after publishing under pressure.** Someone will complain that their beloved home state got a 5.1. The score stays. If new data genuinely changes the situation, update with a visible correction note. Never silently adjust.

3. **Skip a Monday Window email because you're busy.** The Monday cadence is the brand's heartbeat. If the Window doesn't ship on Monday, trust dies a little each time. Write buffer content in advance. Ship on Monday. Always.

4. **Let the visual identity drift.** In Month 4, someone will design a "cool new Instagram template" with gradients and a new font. Say no. The visual discipline is the brand.

5. **Grow the team without briefing them on the editorial standards.** The first hire is more important than the first 100 users. If the first editorial hire doesn't understand the "no paid scores" rule in their bones, the brand starts to decay from the inside.

6. **Become a destination-listing directory.** Feature creep toward "list every Indian destination" kills the editorial quality bar. Stay at 124. Grow slowly. Depth over breadth.

7. **Copy-paste the Goa/Bali aesthetic to drive Instagram engagement.** Ignore the short-term metric. A single sunset reel gets 50K views and kills 2% of the brand's premium positioning. The math is terrible.

8. **Make the mobile app free-with-ads.** Ads on Nakshiq destroy everything the brand stands for. Free forever, no ads. Monetise via Nakshiq Pro subscription when the audience is large enough.

9. **Pitch the brand to investors who want booking-platform unit economics.** Nakshiq is an editorial business. Pitching it as a "travel booking disruptor" to the wrong investors forces the team to become what they're disrupting.

10. **Confuse Nakshiq with Ashish.** The brand has to outlive the founder's voice. Every piece of content should be able to exist without Ashish's name on it. Build editorial systems, not personal dependencies.
