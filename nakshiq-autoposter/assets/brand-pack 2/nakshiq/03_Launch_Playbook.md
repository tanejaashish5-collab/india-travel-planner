## Part One — The Launch Philosophy

Before any tactics, one principle: **Nakshiq launches by earning trust, not by buying attention.** Every Indian travel brand in the category grew by running paid acquisition against keywords like "best places to visit in India" and burning cash on Instagram ads with stock photos. That strategy is available to anyone with a credit card, which is why the category is a swamp of indistinguishable brands. Nakshiq's moat is *editorial credibility* — the audience trusts us because we tell the truth and publish scores nobody else has. A launch strategy that undermines credibility (paying for reviews, hyping mediocre destinations, running misleading ads) destroys the brand before it has one.

The launch approach instead: **seed slowly with the right audience, let the Nakshiq Score become vocabulary inside that audience, and watch it spread outward through word of mouth.** This is how Cred launched (to a small audience of credit-card-paying urban millennials who valued the design and the discipline). This is how Substack launched (to a small audience of writers and readers who valued editorial independence). This is how Kinfolk, Cereal, and Monocle built their publications. **Small + premium + disciplined + patient = category-defining brand.** Big + mass + paid + fast = category-participating brand. Pick one.

The specific tactical bet: **use Ashish's existing Chanakya Sutra audience as the first 1,000 users.** It's the warmest audience available, it already trusts the Ashish worldview (the Chanakya content has been building that trust for months), it overlaps demographically with Nakshiq's target (urban Indian, premium, values intelligence over hype), and it costs nothing. The Chanakya audience is the single highest-leverage distribution asset the founder owns and is currently using it for a separate brand — but a *coordinated* crossover can seed Nakshiq without compromising either brand.

## Part Two — The 30-60-90 Day Launch Plan

### Day 0 — Pre-launch lockdown (this week)

Before any content, before any announcement, before any soft launch, these seven things must be done and verified:

1. **All seven domains registered** (done — nakshiq.com/.in/.app/.travel/.co + two defensive variants)
2. **All six primary social handles claimed** (done — IG, X, YouTube, TikTok, LinkedIn, Threads)
3. **Trademark filings initiated** in Classes 9, 39, 42 with an Indian IP attorney (target: Lex Orbis, K&S Partners, or Obhan & Associates). DPIIT recognition for the startup reduces filing fees from ₹9,000/class to ₹4,500/class — apply through Startup India if not already done.
4. **Mobile app rebranded** from "India Travel Planner" to Nakshiq throughout — splash screen, app icon, onboarding copy, "About" section, all strings audited. Use the `nakshiq-appicon.svg` as the launch icon.
5. **Existing web app updated** at `web-blond-zeta.vercel.app/en` — hero copy rewritten in Nakshiq voice, logo lockup in the header, color system applied, Score examples visible on the homepage. This becomes `nakshiq.in` (or `.com` once you confirm the .com registration is yours).
6. **Email capture live on the homepage** — single field, single button: "Get the first 10 destination scores free. Nakshiq launches in [weeks]." No other form fields. This seeds the soft-launch list.
7. **Legal entity** — Nakshiq operates as a product brand of IMPRESSA DE ARTISTE PTY LIMITED for now. When Indian operations require, register a Private Limited in India (`Nakshiq Technologies Pvt Ltd` or similar) through Startup India fast-track. Attorney can handle this in parallel with trademarks.

**This is Week 0. Nothing else happens until these seven things are verified.**

### Days 1-30 — Soft launch (Friends & Family & Chanakya)

**Theme:** *"The people who already trust me get it first."*

**Goals:**
- 500 verified email signups
- 50 active beta users on the mobile app
- 10 destination pages peer-reviewed by people who have actually been there recently
- One single piece of content that earns organic pickup

**Week 1 — The Private Launch**

No public announcement. Instead, a personal message to a small seed list (30-50 people) selected from:
- Ashish's personal network (other Senior BAs, the Canberra Indian community, ex-colleagues at ACT Health and other agencies)
- Aurore's network (fellow Senior BAs, wider Canberra contacts)
- Family & close friends in India who travel frequently
- A small subset of the Chanakya Sutra audience (*only the most engaged followers, not the full audience yet*)

The message is personal, not a template blast. Example: *"Hey Rajiv — you know that thing I've been working on. It's called Nakshiq now. It's an intelligent map for India — monthly scores on when to actually go to places, honest reviews, no tourism-board BS. Would love 15 minutes of your time to show it to you before I release it publicly. Link's below, login is your email. Tell me what breaks."*

The ask is feedback, not promotion. Every beta user is asked three questions:
1. Did you actually go to a destination in the last 12 months? If so, which one, and in what month?
2. Was our Score for that destination in that month accurate? Be brutal.
3. What's the one thing we're missing that would make you use this before your next trip?

Feedback captured in a structured form. Bugs and broken copy fixed within 48 hours. The tight feedback loop with 50 warm users is worth more than 5,000 cold signups.

**Week 2 — The Editorial Cornerstone**

Publish ONE piece of cornerstone content on the Nakshiq blog that will become the launch artefact. Recommended topic:

> **"The Honest Map of India: 31 Destinations and the Truth About When to Visit Them"**

A single long-form article (3,000-4,000 words) that covers 31 destinations — one for every day of the launch month — with the Nakshiq Score for each, the verdict, and one specific tradeoff per destination nobody else publishes. This article is:
- The proof-of-work that Nakshiq has editorial standards
- The SEO anchor that Google will eventually rank for *"best time to visit [destination] India"*
- The share asset that beta users will forward to friends
- The press angle (journalists love "31 destinations" format)

**Week 3 — The Founder Announcement**

Ashish writes a single long-form founder post — NOT a press release, NOT a launch video, just a personal piece of writing — that explains why Nakshiq exists. Use the founder origin story from the Brand Bible (Part Six) as the backbone. Publish in three places simultaneously:
1. Nakshiq blog as the first public post
2. LinkedIn as a long-form article (Ashish's primary professional network)
3. Substack or Medium as a cross-post for broader reach

Key principle: **don't announce the product, announce the problem.** Most founder launch posts say "I built X, please try it." The ones that spread say "here's a problem I've been obsessed with for years, and here's what I'm doing about it." Read the Superhuman launch post, the Stripe launch post, the Basecamp/37signals launch posts — all of them start with the problem, not the product.

**Week 4 — First Public Signal**

One piece of content on each of Ashish's existing public channels that references Nakshiq *in context*, not as a promotion:
- **Chanakya Sutra YouTube Short:** A strategic crossover that uses a Chanakya Niti principle to explain why Indian travel research is broken and how Nakshiq solves it. Example: *"Chanakya said 'before you act, know the season.' Most Indians pick their travel month based on Instagram. Here's what the old strategist would do instead."* This is a legitimate crossover because the Chanakya channel is about strategic thinking and Nakshiq is about travel strategy.
- **LinkedIn post:** A work-context post about product building — *"What I learned building a travel app that refuses to be a booking platform."* Positions Nakshiq as a product story, not a consumer pitch.
- **Twitter/X:** A 10-tweet thread about the single insight — *"Travel discovery in India is broken because nobody scores by month. Here's what happens when you do."* Thread structure: hook → evidence → the score system → the examples → the soft CTA.

Expected outcome by Day 30: ~500 email signups, ~50 active beta users, one strong piece of content that has 5-10K views in the right audiences, and initial feedback on the product.

**Theme:** *"Nakshiq is live, and the editorial is the proof."*

**Goals:**

The product is now publicly available. Nakshiq moves from "coming soon" to "open." The announcement is coordinated across three channels in one day:

3. **India tech press outreach** — one week before the public launch, Ashish sends personalised pitches to three journalists (NOT a press release). Target list:
   - **Moulishree Srivastava** (The Ken) — covers consumer internet and Indian startups
   - **Pratishtha Bagai** (YourStory) — covers product-led startups
   - **Sneha Goswami** (Morning Context) — covers the crossover of tech and culture
   - **Sonal Khetarpal** (Mint) — covers travel tech and consumer brands
   
   The pitch angle is NOT "new travel app launched." The angle is: *"A former federal contractor built an honest travel scoring system for India — here's why tourism boards are going to hate it."* Reporters want a thesis, not a product.

**Week 6 — The Reddit Play**

Reddit is the single most powerful platform for Nakshiq's target audience that is also the most dangerous to get wrong. The Indian travel subreddits (`r/IndiaTravel`, `r/Ladakh`, `r/HimachalPradesh`, `r/NorthEastIndia`, `r/india`) have strict no-self-promotion rules and will ban a founder who launches with a "check out my app" post.

The right play: *Ashish builds a Reddit presence BEFORE launching there.* Weeks 1-5, Ashish participates in travel threads as a knowledgeable community member, answering specific questions about destinations he knows. By Week 6, he has comment karma, a track record of useful answers, and moderator trust. Only then does he post a single thoughtful piece — *"I've been working on a destination scoring system for India. Here's what I learned about Spiti. Feedback wanted."* — which includes the Nakshiq Score methodology, one destination page, and an honest request for criticism. The CTA is not "sign up." The CTA is "tell me what I got wrong about your home state."

This Reddit play alone can deliver 1,000-2,000 highly engaged signups if executed correctly. Done wrong, it gets Nakshiq banned from every travel subreddit for a year.

**Week 7 — The Editorial Drumbeat Begins**

Nakshiq publishes its first recurring editorial feature: **The Window**.

The Window is a weekly publication (every Monday at 8 AM IST) that answers one question: *"Where should you go in the next six weeks, and where should you not?"* Each issue covers 3 destinations in Peak Window, 2 destinations to skip, and one longer read. It is distributed by:
- Email newsletter (primary — the list you've been building)
- Published on the Nakshiq blog
- Cross-posted as a thread on X/Twitter
- Posted as a single long image-post on Instagram

The Window is the content loop that will drive retention for the next 24 months. It is Nakshiq's equivalent of Morning Brew's daily newsletter, The Ken's Monday launch, or Stratechery's Tuesday long-form. Consistency is non-negotiable. **Published every Monday without exception, forever.**

**Week 8 — Partnership Outreach**

Begin quiet outreach to 5-10 complementary Indian brands for eventual cross-promotion. NOT booking platforms (conflict of interest). NOT tourism boards (credibility compromise). Instead:
- **Premium luggage brands** (Mokobara, Uppercase) — same target audience, different product
- **Outdoor gear brands** (Wildcraft Premium, Decathlon's premium line, Trek Kit India) — same use case
- **Travel-adjacent publications** (Cereal India if it existed, Homegrown, The Established) — same editorial sensibility
- **Auto brands' adventure divisions** (Mahindra Scorpio-N, Maruti Jimny marketing) — overlap with road-trip audience

The ask is *editorial collaboration*, not paid partnership. "Our audience is your audience. Let's produce one piece of content together that serves them." Mokobara publishes a "Packing for Spiti in May" guide powered by Nakshiq's Score and conditions data. Mahindra uses Nakshiq's road condition data for their adventure microsite. These are zero-cost credibility multipliers.

Expected outcome by Day 60: ~5,000 email signups, ~500 active app users, 3 press mentions, first 2-3 editorial partnerships in discussion, Reddit footprint established, The Window running weekly.

### Days 61-90 — Growth (Community + First Viral Moment)

**Theme:** *"Turn users into co-authors and generate one moment that travels."*

**Goals:**
- 20,000 email signups
- 2,500 active mobile app users
- 100+ user-submitted Field Notes (the first moat component)
- One single piece of content that genuinely goes viral within the Indian travel audience

**Week 9 — Launch Nakshiq Field Notes**

Open the Field Notes programme. This is the user-generated layer that will eventually become Nakshiq's editorial moat. The programme works like this:

- Any user who has actually been to a destination in the last 90 days can submit a Field Note: 3-5 sentences of first-person observation, one photo, and one specific detail (the homestay that was closed, the road that was washed out, the restaurant that was better than expected).
- Every Field Note is reviewed by a Nakshiq editor before publishing. Rejected notes get a personal reply explaining why.
- Published Field Notes carry the contributor's name, location, and the date of their visit.
- Top contributors are invited to become "Nakshiq Local" — a small group (aim: 20 by Day 90) who get early access to features, occasional free swag, and direct credit as Regional Experts.

The Field Notes programme is the single most important growth lever after Week 8. It scales content without scaling editorial cost, it creates a status economy inside the audience (people want their Field Note published), and it makes Nakshiq the only platform with verified, recent, first-person ground truth.

**Week 10 — The Skip List**

Publish the single most divisive piece of content Nakshiq will produce in its first 90 days: **The Skip List 2026** — a public list of the 15 most overrated travel destinations in India, with Nakshiq Scores explaining why.

Examples (TBD based on actual scores, not cherry-picked):
- Manali in peak season (crowds + traffic gridlock = 5.2)
- Gulmarg in June (monsoon + low activity = 4.8)
- Shimla on weekends (day-tripper chaos = 5.5)
- Goa North in December (overpriced + overcrowded = 5.0)
- McLeod Ganj in April (pre-monsoon heat + tourist crush = 5.8)

This is the content that goes viral. Overrated-destination lists are shareable because they give people permission to articulate something they already felt. *"Finally, someone said it."* The Skip List becomes Nakshiq's signature editorial move — done annually, updated quarterly, defended rigorously. Tourism boards will complain. That complaint is the marketing.

Publication strategy:
- Published on the Nakshiq blog as the cornerstone piece for the month
- Threaded on X/Twitter (10-15 tweet thread, one destination per tweet)
- Instagram carousel (15 slides, one destination per slide, Nakshiq visual identity throughout)
- Sent to the email list with the subject line *"The 15 destinations we're telling people to skip this year"*
- Pitched to press as a follow-up story — *"The new Indian travel platform that refuses to be diplomatic"*

Expected result: 20K-100K impressions across channels, one or two angry responses from tourism boards (which become their own content), 500-2,000 new email signups from the viral moment.

**Week 11 — The Paid Acquisition Experiment**

Only now, with organic traction established, does Nakshiq experiment with paid acquisition. The budget is small (₹50K-1L for the first test) and the targeting is extremely narrow:

- **Instagram/Facebook Ads** targeting: urban Indian users aged 28-45, interests in travel + premium brands (Mokobara, Raw Mango, Nykaa, Cred, The Ken), who have engaged with travel content in the last 30 days
- **Creative:** NOT a product screenshot. A single Nakshiq Score card with a strong hook. *"Manali in May: 5.2. Here's what we'd go to instead."* → leads to a specific destination page
- **Goal:** Validate the CAC. If one signup costs under ₹40, paid acquisition is viable. If it costs ₹200+, the brand is still in editorial-only mode and we reallocate to more Skip List-type viral content.

The principle: **paid acquisition amplifies what's already working. It does not create something that isn't working.** If organic conversion is weak, more budget is worse, not better.

**Week 12 — The First Review**

By Week 12, Nakshiq has enough data, enough users, and enough editorial output that a reflective "first 90 days" review is justified. Ashish writes one post — *"What we learned shipping Nakshiq to 20,000 people"* — that is equal parts founder transparency and product roadmap. This post:
- Recaps the metrics that matter (signups, active users, Field Notes submitted, The Window subscribers)
- Names the things that didn't work (honest post-mortem on failed features or missed assumptions)
- Announces what's next (the mobile app if not yet launched, Nakshiq Pro pricing plans, the Atlas expansion to new regions)
- Asks the audience what they want next

Founder transparency posts are the cheapest, most effective retention tool in existence. They tell the audience *"you are not a metric to us, you are a community,"* and they generate inbound feedback that shapes the roadmap.

Expected outcome by Day 90: ~20,000 email signups, ~2,500 active users, ~100 published Field Notes, one viral Skip List moment, a small paid acquisition funnel validated or killed, a founder review post that sets the tone for the next quarter.

## Part Three — Channel Playbooks

Each channel has a specific purpose in the Nakshiq system. None of them are treated equally. Below: what each channel is FOR, what it is NOT for, and the specific discipline.

### Email / The Window newsletter

**The most important channel. Full stop.**

Email is the only channel Nakshiq controls. Instagram can ban the account. Google can delist the blog. X can change its algorithm overnight. The email list is the one asset that can never be taken away.

**Cadence:** The Window weekly (Monday 8 AM IST) + occasional special editions (seasonal guides, Skip List annual, founder notes).

**Tone:** The closest to the Nakshiq voice of any channel. Short paragraphs, strong verdicts, data front and centre. Every email opens with one line of the week's headline call. Example opener: *"Spiti's window opens Thursday. Here's what to do about it, and here's what you should not do about Gulmarg this week."*

**Design:** Plain text with one optional hero image. NOT an HTML template with 15 sections. The Morning Brew aesthetic, not the MakeMyTrip promotional email aesthetic. Easy to read on a phone in 90 seconds.

**Tools:** Ghost (if you want the CMS + newsletter combined), Buttondown (if you want clean minimal), ConvertKit (if you want creator-tier), Substack (if you want distribution built in). Recommendation: **Ghost**, because it lets Nakshiq control the brand and it gives you a native membership/paid tier when Nakshiq Pro launches.

**Growth target:** 20K by Day 90, 100K by Day 365.

### Instagram

**The showcase channel, not the growth channel.**

Instagram's role is to *reinforce brand credibility* for people who have already heard about Nakshiq from another source. Somebody sees The Window mentioned on Twitter, checks Nakshiq's Instagram to vibe-check whether it's legit, sees a beautiful feed of destination scores and topographic imagery, and converts to an email signup. Instagram is the credibility filter, not the front door.

**Content pillars (4 post types on rotation):**

1. **Score Cards** — single-image posts of a Nakshiq Score with verdict. Clean typography on Bone background. Each card is a mini-poster. One per week minimum.
2. **Skip List Items** — single destinations from the Skip List with the score and one specific reason. These perform well because they're contrarian.
3. **Field Notes** — contributor photos with a 3-sentence caption. Credit the contributor prominently. These create social proof for the programme.
4. **The Window crosspost** — once a week, a carousel version of The Window newsletter with the top 3 destinations in Peak Window.

**Format discipline:** NO reels in Month 1. NO Stories about "behind the scenes." NO quote posts with generic travel wisdom. NO hashtag soup. The feed looks like a publication, not a personal account. If in doubt, hold the post.

**Growth target:** 5K followers by Day 90, 25K by Day 365. Followers are not the metric — engagement rate is. Aim for 5%+ engagement rate (which is Cereal/Kinfolk territory, not Tripoto territory).

### X (Twitter)

**The discussion channel.**

X is where Nakshiq's editorial positioning gets debated, where The Skip List gets screenshotted and spread, where journalists discover the brand, and where the founder voice lives. Not a place for polished marketing — a place for opinions, threads, and conversations.

**Content formula:**
- 3-5 posts per week minimum
- At least one long thread per week (Score methodology, Skip List item, Field Note deep dive, founder insight)
- Frequent replies to travel conversations happening on the timeline — Ashish is a participant in Indian travel Twitter, not just a broadcaster
- Screenshots of Nakshiq Score cards from the Instagram feed get reposted here with sharper commentary

**Founder voice:** Ashish posts from both `@nakshiq` (brand account) and his personal account, with clear separation. Brand account is editorial. Personal account is founder transparency.

### YouTube

**Optional in Months 1-3. Essential by Month 6.**

YouTube is where Nakshiq eventually builds its long-form editorial authority — the India-travel-honesty version of what Wendover Productions is to aviation or what Marques Brownlee is to consumer tech. But producing YouTube content at Nakshiq's quality bar (scripted, visually refined, editorially rigorous) is expensive in time. Months 1-3 use YouTube only for low-effort experiments: the occasional founder video, a Score reveal animation, a Skip List thread converted to video.

**By Month 4-6:** commit to one 8-12 minute video per month on a specific destination or theme. Example: *"Why October Spiti is the trap everyone falls into."* Production bar: voiceover, tasteful B-roll, on-screen data from Nakshiq, no clickbait thumbnails, no face-cam.

**Chanakya Sutra crossover:** there's a real opportunity to cross-pollinate the Chanakya Sutra channel's audience into Nakshiq at specific moments — for example, a Chanakya-themed video about strategic travel decisions. Do this sparingly (2-3 times per year maximum) to avoid diluting either brand.

### LinkedIn

**The founder's channel.**

LinkedIn is where Ashish tells the Nakshiq product story in founder voice. Posts are not about destinations — they're about the product, the decisions, the lessons learned, the hard tradeoffs. This audience (Senior BAs, product managers, federal contractors, Ashish's professional network) is not the traveller audience, but it is the audience that can generate early word-of-mouth referrals, angel investment interest, and potential hires.

**Cadence:** 1-2 posts per week from Ashish's personal account. The brand has a LinkedIn company page but it's updated less frequently (monthly).

**Topics:** product-building stories, design decisions (why Instrument Serif), editorial discipline as moat, the "refuse to become a booking platform" decision, hiring announcements, press mentions.

### TikTok / Instagram Reels

**Skip in Months 1-3. Consider in Month 6.**

Short-form video is the highest-potential channel and the highest-risk channel. It rewards personality and algorithm-friendly content, which can clash with Nakshiq's editorial discipline. If Nakshiq does enter this space, it's with one specific format: **the Score Reveal** — a 15-second video that shows a destination, reveals its Nakshiq Score with the signature ink-stamp animation and Tibetan-bowl audio, and delivers the verdict in one line. Consistent format, brand-right tone, easy to scale.

This is a Month 6 decision, not a Month 1 decision.

### Reddit

**The trust-building channel.** (See Week 6 of the launch plan above.)

Not a broadcast channel. A participation channel. Ashish is a member of travel subreddits who happens to run Nakshiq, not a brand account trying to extract signups. The rules of engagement:
- Never post "check out my app" content
- Always lead with useful answers to other people's questions
- Reference Nakshiq only when specifically relevant and only when adding value
- Disclose the affiliation clearly every time
- Accept criticism publicly and graciously

Reddit can deliver the highest-quality users of any channel if done right. It can also ban the brand for life if done wrong. Handle with discipline.

## Part Four — Content Pillars

Every piece of Nakshiq content falls into one of six pillars. Content that doesn't fit a pillar doesn't get made.

### Pillar One — The Verdict

Destination-specific content with Nakshiq Scores. The core product content. Example formats:

- *"Spiti, May: 9.2. The Window Opens."*
- *"Gulmarg, June: 4.8. Here's Why to Skip It."*
- *"Tawang in October: 8.1. What the Permits Don't Tell You."*

Every Verdict piece has: the score, the verdict word (Peak Window / Strong / Good / etc), the one specific tradeoff, and one photo with caption.

### Pillar Two — The Skip List

Contrarian content about overrated destinations. The signature viral format. Published once a quarter for individual destinations, once a year for the full Skip List. Tourism boards will complain. The complaint is marketing.

### Pillar Three — Field Notes

User-contributed first-person observations from recent trips. 3-5 sentences plus one photo plus one specific detail. Reviewed and published by Nakshiq editors. Creates both content and status economy.

### Pillar Four — The Window

Weekly newsletter and its Twitter/Instagram adaptations. 3 Peak Window destinations + 2 Skip recommendations + 1 longer read. Every Monday. Forever.

### Pillar Five — The Methodology

Content about how Nakshiq actually works — the scoring system, the editorial process, the data sources. This content builds trust with the skeptical audience and becomes the primary asset for press pitches. Example: *"How we score 124 destinations every month — and why we refuse to use algorithms."*

### Pillar Six — The Founder Notes

Ashish's personal writing about building Nakshiq, the decisions, the mistakes, the lessons. Published on the blog + LinkedIn. Monthly cadence.

**What Nakshiq does NOT make:**
- Generic "top 10 things to do in X" listicles
- "Things nobody tells you about India" clickbait
- Influencer vlogs
- Aspirational travel diaries
- Curated reels of sunsets
- Tourism board partnerships that require editorial compromise
- Sponsored destination features
- AI-generated destination descriptions

## Part Five — The First 100 Users (Tactical)

The first 100 users are the most important 100 users Nakshiq will ever have. They are the people who will shape the product, provide the first Field Notes, generate the first word-of-mouth referrals, and either validate or kill the launch thesis. Getting these 100 right is worth more than the next 10,000.

**The sourcing map — where the first 100 come from:**

| Source | Target count | Approach |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| Ashish's personal network (Canberra + India) | 15-20 | Direct personal message, individual invites |
| Aurore's network (Senior BA community) | 5-10 | Via Aurore, one warm intro per person |
| Chanakya Sutra most-engaged followers | 25-30 | A quiet DM to the top 50 most-engaged followers — "I'm building something different, want to try it?" |
| Ex-colleagues from ACT Health, ATO, DoHA, etc | 10-15 | LinkedIn DM, Nakshiq pitched as a product story, not a travel product |
| Friends-of-friends referrals from the above | 15-20 | "Who in your network would love this? Mind forwarding?" |
| Indian travel Twitter participants (organic) | 5-10 | Built slowly via replies over Weeks 1-4 |
| Cold outreach to 3-5 specific targets | 3-5 | Travel journalists, product Twitter personalities, known India travel writers |

The goal is a balanced mix: people who will actually use the app (travellers), people who will talk about it (writers/journalists), people who will give honest feedback (product folks), and people whose endorsement carries weight (the trusted names in the target community).

**What every first-100 user gets:**
- Direct personal onboarding from Ashish
- A 15-minute call within 7 days of signup (for the first 30)
- A spot in a private WhatsApp or Discord called "Nakshiq Founders" where they can submit feedback directly
- First access to every new feature
- Permanent recognition as a founding user
- Their name listed on a public "Founding 100" page on the Nakshiq website

The Founding 100 page itself becomes a marketing asset — people want their name on it, and seeing real names (not faceless testimonials) creates credibility for later users.

## Part Six — Partnerships & Ecosystem

### The five brand tiers Nakshiq can partner with (without compromising editorial integrity)

**Tier 1 — Adjacent premium brands (highest priority)**
Same audience, different product, zero conflict of interest.
- Mokobara (luggage), Uppercase (luggage), Wildcraft Premium, Decathlon Adventure, DSLR/camera brands, adventure apparel, premium accessories
- Partnership format: co-produced content (guides, packing lists, destination playbooks), cross-promotion to each other's newsletters, joint Instagram content
- Ask: zero-cost editorial collaboration, no commission structure

**Tier 2 — Lifestyle publications (credibility multipliers)**
- Homegrown, The Established, Paper Planes, Condé Nast Traveller India (editorial, not advertorial), Architectural Digest India
- Partnership format: guest columns, contributor exchanges, mutual editorial features
- Ask: editorial cross-promotion, not paid

**Tier 3 — Auto brands' adventure divisions**
- Mahindra (Scorpio-N, Thar, XUV700 adventure marketing), Maruti Jimny, Hero Xpulse, Royal Enfield Himalayan
- Partnership format: Nakshiq's road condition data powers their adventure microsites; they feature Nakshiq on their owner communication
- Ask: data licensing deal or co-marketing moment

**Tier 4 — State tourism boards (handle with extreme care)**
- Himachal, Uttarakhand, Ladakh, Arunachal, Meghalaya Tourism
- Partnership format: **only** data sharing (Nakshiq can use their official road/weather/permit data; they can cite Nakshiq's editorial as independent third-party validation when it's positive)
- Ask: zero-money, data-only. **Never accept payment or free hosting for coverage.**
- The red line: Nakshiq's scores are never modified in response to tourism board relationships. If they ask for that, the relationship ends.

**Tier 5 — Educational and cultural institutions**
- IIM travel clubs, IIT alumni travel groups, Ashoka and Krea travel societies, Indian foreign service cultural programmes, Indian embassy cultural affairs (abroad)
- Partnership format: talks, workshops, cultural programming
- Ask: brand awareness in high-quality audiences

### The brands Nakshiq must never partner with

- OTAs (MakeMyTrip, Booking.com, Goibibo, Agoda) — conflict of interest
- Sponsored destination content marketplaces
- Influencer marketplaces that require destination photoshoots with pre-approved angles
- Any brand that requires Nakshiq to modify a Score or Verdict

## Part Seven — Metrics That Actually Matter

Not every metric is worth tracking. The ones that matter for Nakshiq, in priority order:

### North Star Metric

**The Window Monday open rate.** This single number is the brand's heartbeat. If The Window has a 50%+ open rate week after week, Nakshiq is healthy. If it drops below 30%, something is wrong. The Window is the canary.

### Secondary metrics (weekly review)

### Metrics Nakshiq does NOT chase

- Total downloads (can be gamed)
- Total followers (vanity)
- Time on site (meaningless for a utility product)
- Pages per session (we want fewer pages, more value)
- Bounce rate (users who get their answer quickly bounce — that's success, not failure)
